# About The Thekua Company

## What We Are

The Thekua Company is an online store selling handmade Thekua — a traditional fried sweet from the Mithila region of northern Bihar and southern Nepal. Every pack is made fresh after each order by Mithila homemakers, using only three to four ingredients: whole wheat flour (chakki atta), jaggery or sugar, pure desi ghee, and dried grated coconut (in the Mithila recipe). No preservatives. No factory. No pre-made stock.

## Our Mission

To bring Thekua back — not as a festival food reserved for Chhath Puja, but as a wholesome, everyday snack. Thekua was historically an everyday food in Mithila homes, kept in a steel dabba on the kitchen shelf and eaten with chai. Packaged snacks displaced it from this role over the past three decades. The Thekua Company is rebuilding the market and the infrastructure it never had.

## What Is Thekua?

Thekua is a traditional fried sweet from Mithila made from:
- **Chakki atta** (stone-ground whole wheat flour)
- **Block jaggery (gur)** — dissolved into syrup before mixing
- **Pure desi ghee** — in the dough and as the frying medium
- **Dried grated coconut** — for texture (the Mithila-specific detail)

It is pressed using a hand-carved wooden mould called a **sancha**, which imprints a floral or geometric pattern. The word "thekua" comes from "thokna" — to hammer or press — a direct reference to pressing dough onto the mould.

Thekua is fried slowly in desi ghee on a low flame for 12–15 minutes. The result is firm, deep golden brown, with a shelf life of 60-75 days in an airtight container at room temperature. No preservatives required.

## The Homemakers

**Neetu Yadav and Geeta Singh** are the co-founders and the homemakers who make every batch.

Both grew up in Mithila making Thekua the way every woman in their community did — by hand, from memory, without measuring. Not as a hobby or cultural exercise, but as a daily act. The Thekua Company didn't give them a new skill; it gave their existing skill a market.

- **Neetu Yadav** grew up in a house where the steel dabba on the kitchen shelf was never empty. Thekua wasn't a heritage food — it was just food. She co-founded The Thekua Company to give that everyday food the everyday market it always deserved.
- **Geeta Singh** never learned thekua from a written recipe. She learned by watching women who had been doing it for decades, getting it wrong until her hands got it right. The dough consistency, the ghee temperature, the moment it's ready — none of it has a timer. Her hands know.

Every batch is made to the same standard they were taught. No batch production. No factory shortcuts.

## Our Story

One afternoon over chai, a question came up that wouldn't go away: *"Why did Thekua, once a staple of every home, become a sweet reserved for just one time of the year?"*

The recipe hadn't gone anywhere. The homemakers hadn't stopped making it. What was missing was a market.

So they built one.

The moulds used to press Thekua in Mithila are carved from sanjehi clay, hand-carved, passed from one maker to the next. Wheat, jaggery, and ghee go in. What comes out carries the shape of hands that have been doing this for generations.
